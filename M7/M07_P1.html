Why Evaluation Matters

The Central Challenge of LLM Evaluation

  • LLMs generate open-ended, probabilistic text — there is rarely a single correct answer.
  • Traditional software testing (pass/fail unit tests) cannot capture whether a response is useful, accurate, or safe.
  • Without systematic evaluation, teams are flying blind: shipping features they cannot verify and missing regressions they cannot detect.
  • Good evaluation is the foundation of reliable AI.

The Three Layers of the Problem

  • Correctness: Does the model produce factually accurate, on-task outputs?
  • Quality: Is the output coherent, well-structured, and appropriate in tone?
  • Safety & Alignment: Does the model avoid harmful, biased, or policy-violating outputs?
  • Each layer requires different metrics, benchmarks, and evaluation strategies.

The Evaluation Landscape

  • Evaluation methods fall on a spectrum from fully automated to fully human.
  • Automated metrics are fast and cheap but can miss nuance and meaning.
  • Human evaluation is the gold standard but is slow, expensive, and inconsistent at scale.
  • The field is converging on a middle path: LLM-as-a-Judge — using another LLM to score outputs at scale.

Classical NLP Metrics

Reference-Based Metrics

  • Early NLP evaluation relied on comparing model output to one or more reference texts.
  • The core assumption: a good output should closely resemble a human-written reference.
  • This works well for constrained tasks (translation, summarization) but breaks down for open-ended generation.
  • Key insight: two correct answers to the same question can share almost no vocabulary.

BLEU Score

  • BLEU (Bilingual Evaluation Understudy) measures the overlap of n-grams between a generated text and reference texts.
  • Computes a geometric mean of n-gram precisions (n = 1, 2, 3, 4) with a brevity penalty for short outputs. \text{BLEU} = \text{BP} \cdot \exp\!\left(\sum_{n=1}^{N} w_n \log p_n\right)
  • Designed for machine translation; widely misapplied to other tasks.
  • Weakness: Completely ignores semantics — a paraphrase scores near zero.

ROUGE

  • ROUGE (Recall-Oriented Understudy for Gisting Evaluation) focuses on recall of reference content.
  • ROUGE-N: Overlap of n-grams between generated and reference text.
  • ROUGE-L: Longest common subsequence — captures fluency and word order.
  • ROUGE-S: Skip-bigram co-occurrence, allowing gaps between matched words.
  • Dominant metric for summarization but shares BLEU’s semantic blindness.

METEOR & CIDEr

  • METEOR improves on BLEU by incorporating synonymy, stemming, and paraphrase matching via WordNet.
  • Balances precision and recall with a harmonic mean, adding a fragmentation penalty for non-contiguous matches.
  • CIDEr (Consensus-based Image Description Evaluation): designed for image captioning.
    • Weights n-grams by TF-IDF across the dataset — common phrases count less.
    • Better correlates with human judgment in vision-language tasks.

Perplexity

  • Perplexity measures how well a language model predicts a held-out test corpus. \text{PPL}(X) = \exp\!\left(-\frac{1}{N}\sum_{i=1}^{N} \log P(x_i \mid x_{<i})\right)
  • Lower perplexity = the model is less “surprised” by real text = better language modeling.
  • Useful for comparing models of the same architecture on the same tokenizer.
  • Not useful for evaluating whether a model gives the right answer to a question.

BERTScore

  • BERTScore replaces n-gram overlap with contextual embeddings from BERT.
  • Computes token-level cosine similarity between generated and reference tokens.
  • Reports Precision, Recall, and F1 in embedding space — semantically aware. \text{BERTScore}_F = \frac{2 \cdot P_{BERT} \cdot R_{BERT}}{P_{BERT} + R_{BERT}}
  • Significantly better correlation with human judgments than BLEU/ROUGE on many benchmarks.
  • Still reference-dependent — requires a gold-standard answer.

LLM Benchmarks

What is a Benchmark?

  • A benchmark is a curated dataset of tasks with known correct answers used to measure model capability on a standardized, reproducible scale.
  • Benchmarks enable apples-to-apples comparison across models, versions, and providers.
  • They are organized by capability: knowledge, reasoning, coding, instruction-following, safety, etc.
  • Critical caveat: A model can score well on a benchmark without being useful in your specific application.

Knowledge & Reasoning Benchmarks

  • MMLU (Massive Multitask Language Understanding): 57 subjects from elementary math to professional law. Tests breadth of world knowledge.
  • ARC (AI2 Reasoning Challenge): Grade-school science questions designed to require reasoning beyond pattern matching.
  • HellaSwag: Commonsense NLI — pick the most plausible sentence completion from four options. Tests grounded reasoning.
  • TruthfulQA: Probes whether a model generates truthful answers to questions that humans commonly answer wrongly.

Coding & Math Benchmarks

  • HumanEval: 164 Python programming problems with unit tests. Measures pass@k — the probability that at least one of k samples passes all tests.
  • MBPP (Mostly Basic Python Problems): 500 crowd-sourced Python problems spanning data structures, algorithms, and math.
  • GSM8K: 8,500 grade-school math word problems requiring multi-step arithmetic. A strong test of chain-of-thought reasoning.
  • MATH: Competition-level math problems across 7 subjects; much harder than GSM8K.

Instruction-Following & Alignment

  • MT-Bench: 80 multi-turn conversation questions across writing, roleplay, reasoning, and coding — scored by GPT-4.
  • AlpacaEval: Automatic evaluation of instruction-following using win rates against a reference model.
  • IFEval (Instruction-Following Evaluation): Tests strict adherence to verifiable formatting and content instructions.
  • BBH (BIG-Bench Hard): 23 tasks from BIG-Bench that larger models still find challenging, requiring chain-of-thought.

The Data Contamination Problem

  • Contamination occurs when benchmark test data appears — intentionally or not — in a model’s training corpus.
  • A contaminated model memorizes answers rather than generalizing, inflating its benchmark score.
  • Indicators: suspiciously high scores on old benchmarks, performance drops on held-out versions.
  • Most published SOTA numbers should be treated with healthy skepticism.
  • Mitigation: use dynamic benchmarks, held-out evaluation sets, and contamination detection tools.

Benchmark Saturation

  • As models improve, they saturate benchmarks — GPT-4 class models score >90% on MMLU.
  • A saturated benchmark provides no signal for differentiating frontier models.
  • The field responds by creating harder benchmarks: GPQA (expert-level science), FrontierMath, SWE-bench.
  • Key takeaway: benchmark selection must be matched to the capability you are trying to measure.

LLM-as-a-Judge

The Limits of Automated Metrics

  • Automated metrics (BLEU, ROUGE, BERTScore) require reference texts — expensive to create for every task.
  • They cannot evaluate: tone, helpfulness, factual consistency, reasoning quality, or safety.
  • Human evaluation is the gold standard but costs $10–$50 per annotation and is slow.
  • Solution: Use a powerful LLM to act as an automated, scalable human-quality judge.

How LLM-as-a-Judge Works

  • A judge LLM (typically GPT-4o or a fine-tuned evaluator) is given:
    1. The original prompt / question
    2. The model’s response (and optionally a reference answer)
    3. A rubric describing what “good” looks like
  • The judge returns a score (e.g., 1–5) and an explanation for its rating.
  • Every LLM-as-a-Judge has three components: the LLM, the evaluation prompt, and a reference dataset.

Evaluation Criteria Taxonomy

Criterion What it Measures
Correctness Factual accuracy against known ground truth
Helpfulness Does the response address the user’s actual need?
Coherence Logical flow and internal consistency
Conciseness Avoids unnecessary verbosity
Safety Free of harmful, biased, or policy-violating content
Groundedness Claims are supported by provided context (RAG)

Pairwise vs. Pointwise Evaluation

  • Pointwise: The judge scores a single response on a scale. Simple and fast.
  • Pairwise (preference): The judge is shown two responses (A vs. B) and picks the winner. More reliable for subtle quality differences.
  • Pairwise evaluation underlies RLHF (Reinforcement Learning from Human Feedback) and DPO training.
  • Position bias: LLMs tend to prefer the first response shown. Always randomize order and check for consistency.

Known Biases in LLM Judges

  • Verbosity bias: Longer responses are rated higher even when less accurate.
  • Self-preference bias: A judge LLM may favor outputs from the same model family.
  • Authority bias: Responses that cite sources or use confident language are favored.
  • Mitigation: Use chain-of-thought in the judge prompt, calibrate on human-annotated gold sets, report inter-rater agreement scores.

Eval Engineering

What is Eval Engineering?

  • Eval Engineering is the discipline of designing, building, tuning, and operating LLM evaluations as production-grade software.
  • Emerged from observing that data science and software engineering are merging in LLM-based systems.
  • LLM features are built as software and tested like ML systems — requiring a new workflow.
  • Goal: create and maintain high-accuracy evaluations that govern AI systems at scale.
  • Source: Galileo Eval Engineering

The 6-Step Eval Engineering Workflow

Eval Engineering follows the entire lifecycle of an evaluation, from the initial LLM-as-a-Judge prompt to deploying it as a production guardrail.

  1. Create — Build the LLM-as-a-Judge prompt
  2. Tune — Achieve >95% agreement with human experts
  3. Develop — Score development traffic; run CI/CD evals
  4. Fine-tune SLM — Distill the judge into a fast, cheap Small Language Model
  5. Deploy — Run as a real-time production guardrail
  6. Recycle — Feed production failures back into the eval dataset

Step 1 — Creating the LLM-as-a-Judge

  • Start when you identify an eval needed to track a desired behavior from your LLM system.
  • The eval can be: code-based, an off-the-shelf LLM judge, or a fully custom bespoke judge.
  • Three required components:
    • The LLM (e.g., GPT-4o)
    • The prompt used to perform the evaluation
    • A reference dataset to test accuracy against
  • Output: a metric score measuring performance for a given span according to your criteria (e.g., friendliness, conciseness, groundedness).

Step 2 — Tuning the Eval

  • An eval is only as good as its tuning. Without tuning, LLM-based evals achieve approximately 66–68% agreement with human subject matter experts at best.
  • Tuning process:
    1. SME Annotations — Experts review real LLM outputs and score according to eval criteria, establishing a ground truth reference dataset
    2. Prompt Optimization — Prompt engineers tweak the judge prompt and measure F1 scores against the SME reference
    3. Few-Shot Examples — Difficult edge cases are added as few-shot examples to give the judge more explicit instructions
  • Target: >95% accuracy before using the eval in development.

Step 3 — Developing with Evals

  • Once the judge achieves >95% accuracy, begin scoring development traffic.
  • Evals become core engineering tools:
    • Benchmark app performance — Measure specific improvements with A/B tests
    • CI/CD integration — Execute evals on every commit to catch regressions automatically
    • Production monitoring — Run LLM-as-a-judge to score production traffic for daily rollups and casual monitoring
  • Treat evals like unit tests: they must run automatically and block bad deployments.

Step 4 — Fine-Tuning the SLM

  • Most LLM judges are slow and expensive: 2–4 second latency per eval, costs up to $50k/day for millions of spans.
  • Solution: fine-tune a Small Language Model (SLM) on the high-accuracy eval prompt from Step 2.
  • This is where true data science meets engineering — distilling the large judge’s behavior into a 1–3B parameter model.
  • Target: eval latency below 150ms, enabling 100% production traffic coverage.
  • Data science and software engineering converge at this step.

Step 5 — Deploying the Guardrail

  • With an SLM-as-a-judge running at low cost and latency, deploy it as a real-time guardrail.
  • The guardrail scores every production response and can:
    • Block or flag responses below a safety or quality threshold
    • Trigger alerts or fallback logic for high-risk outputs
    • Provide continuous quality dashboards for product teams
  • Evaluation shifts from a testing artifact to an operational control plane.

Step 6 — Recycling Learnings

  • Real-world environments will confound even the most meticulous engineers.
    • Consumer behavior changes
    • Model drift occurs after updates
    • Unanticipated failure modes emerge
  • A system observing 100% of production traffic can quickly capture failure modes into reference datasets.
  • These datasets feed back into Step 2, continuously refining evals or creating new ones.
  • Eval Engineering is a flywheel — not a one-time setup.

Introduction to Multimodal LLMs

From Text to the Real World

  • Standard LLMs process only text. But the world communicates through multiple modalities simultaneously: a doctor reads a scan and a patient report; a factory sensor emits vibration and a maintenance log entry.
  • Combining modalities gives AI systems a richer, more accurate understanding of context.
  • Multimodal LLMs (MLLMs) are AI systems designed to process and integrate diverse data types — text, images, video, audio, and structured signals — in a single unified model.

What is a Multimodal LLM?

  • An MLLM is a sophisticated AI system engineered to handle and decode multimodal data concurrently.
  • The core principle: integration and interplay of varied modalities amplifies model effectiveness.
  • By synthesizing data from multiple sources, MLLMs achieve more comprehensive and precise understanding than any single-modality system.
  • Examples: GPT-4o (text + images + audio), Gemini 1.5 Pro (text + images + video + audio), Llama 3.2 Vision (text + images).

Why Multimodal? The World Sends Rich Signals

  • Healthcare: Analyze MRI scans + clinical notes to improve diagnostic accuracy.
  • Manufacturing: Combine machine sensor readings + maintenance logs + visual inspection images.
  • Business Intelligence: Interpret charts, tables, and text from reports simultaneously.
  • Customer Service: Understand screenshots, voice recordings, and typed messages together.
  • Single-modality systems require multiple separate pipelines; MLLMs offer a unified interface.

MLLM Architecture

The Three-Component Architecture

Every MLLM is built from three fundamental blocks:

  1. Multimodal Input Encoders — Convert raw modality data (pixels, waveforms, tokens) into feature vectors
  2. Feature Fusion Mechanism — Integrate features from all modalities into a shared representation
  3. LLM Backbone + Output Decoder — Reason over the fused representation and generate a response

Understanding these components clarifies both the capabilities and limitations of any MLLM.

Multimodal Input Encoders

  • Text: Embedding layers + transformer encoders (BERT, RoBERTa). Map tokens to continuous vector space.
  • Images: Vision Transformer (ViT) treats image patches as sequences; ResNet learns hierarchical spatial features.
  • Audio: C-Former, HuBERT, BEATs, or Whisper capture temporal acoustic patterns — from basic tones to complex speech.
  • Sequential/Physiological: 1D-CNN + LSTM captures temporal and spatial patterns in EEG, ECG, and sensor streams.
  • Universal Encoders: ImageBind and similar models encode audio, video, depth, thermal, and IMU into a single embedding space.

Feature Fusion Mechanisms

Each encoder produces feature vectors V_{\text{image}}, V_{\text{text}}, V_{\text{audio}}, etc. These must be combined:

  • Early Fusion: Concatenate raw or low-level features before encoding. Captures low-level cross-modal correlations.
  • Intermediate Fusion: Merge features during the extraction phase — each modality contributes its unique properties to a unified representation. (Most common approach)
  • Late Fusion: Combine final per-modality outputs at the decision stage. Good for tasks requiring independent modality reasoning.
  • Joint (Hybrid) Fusion: Merges early, intermediate, and late stages to maximize data utilization.

Feeding Multimodal Features into an LLM

  • Pre-trained LLMs accept text sequences, not raw image or audio tensors.
  • Projection: A linear layer or small MLP maps visual/audio feature vectors into the LLM’s token embedding space. [V_{\text{image}}, V_{\text{text}}, \ldots, V_{\text{audio}}] \xrightarrow{\text{project}} \text{Token Sequence} \rightarrow \text{LLM}
  • Serialization: Multimodal features are arranged as a sequence alongside text tokens and fed into the transformer.
  • The LLM then reasons over this combined token stream using its standard self-attention mechanism.

The Output Decoder

  • Most MLLMs generate text output — captions, answers, summaries, reasoning chains.
  • Image generation models (DALL-E 3, Stable Diffusion) add a visual decoder (e.g., vector-quantized VAE, diffusion model).
  • Any-to-any models (NExT-GPT, Emu3) support generation across all modalities from a single model.
  • The decoder is selected for the target task: text generation, image synthesis, audio output, or multi-modal combinations.

Building MLLMs: Two Architectures

Method A — Unified Embedding Decoder

  • All modalities are projected into the same embedding space as text tokens before being fed into the LLM.
  • The LLM sees a single, unified token sequence containing both text and visual tokens.
  • How it works: A lightweight connector (linear projection or Q-Former) maps vision encoder outputs to token embeddings.
  • Examples: LLaVA, MiniGPT-4, InstructBLIP, Llama 3.2 Vision, Qwen2-VL.
  • Advantage: Simple architecture; leverages the full power of pre-trained LLMs with minimal modification.

Method B — Cross-Modality Attention

  • Visual and text features remain in separate streams. Cross-attention layers allow the text stream to “attend to” visual features.
  • The LLM backbone is modified to include cross-attention gates at every transformer layer.
  • How it works: Visual tokens are keys and values; text tokens are queries — the model selectively pulls visual information where relevant.
  • Examples: Flamingo, NVLM-X, Idefics.
  • Advantage: Better at precise visual localization; scales well to high-resolution and video inputs.

Training: What Gets Frozen?

  • Training from scratch on multimodal data is prohibitively expensive.
  • Standard approach: freeze the pre-trained LLM and train only the connector layers initially.

Stage 2: Instruction tuning Trained Frozen Trained

Evolution of MLLMs: A Timeline

The Foundations (Pre-2021)

  • Word2Vec (2013): Demonstrated that semantic meaning could be encoded in dense vector spaces — the conceptual precursor to multimodal embeddings.
  • ResNet (2015): Enabled deep visual feature learning without vanishing gradients.
  • Transformers (2017): Replaced RNNs with self-attention, enabling parallelizable sequence modeling for text.
  • BERT (2018) / GPT-2 (2019): Pre-trained LLMs showed transfer learning was possible at scale.
  • The stage was set: powerful vision and language models existed separately — the question was how to connect them.

The Connector Era (2021–2022)

  • CLIP (OpenAI, 2021): Trained on 400M image-text pairs using contrastive learning; unified image and text into a shared embedding space. Became the standard vision encoder for MLLMs.
  • DALL-E (2021): First large-scale text-to-image model using transformers over discrete image tokens.
  • Flamingo (DeepMind, 2022): Pioneered the cross-attention architecture for MLLMs; few-shot visual QA.
  • BLIP / BLIP-2 (2022–2023): Introduced Q-Former as an efficient bridge between vision encoders and LLMs.

The Open-Source Explosion (2023)

  • LLaVA (2023): Simple, open-source MLLM using CLIP + LLaMA with a linear projection connector. Demonstrated that a small dataset of GPT-4-generated instruction data was highly effective.
  • MiniGPT-4 (2023): Aligned frozen ViT + BLIP-2 with Vicuna using a single projection layer.
  • InstructBLIP (2023): Instruction-tuned BLIP-2; introduced instruction-aware visual feature extraction via Q-Former.
  • GPT-4V (OpenAI, 2023): Brought vision capabilities to GPT-4; sparked widespread adoption of vision-language AI.

The Proliferation Era (2024)

  • Llama 3.2 Vision (Meta, 2024): First open-weights multimodal Llama; Method A unified embedding with cross-attention variant.
  • NVLM (NVIDIA, 2024): Three-in-one architecture family (decoder-only, cross-attention, hybrid) for frontier performance.
  • Qwen2-VL (Alibaba, 2024): Native dynamic resolution with Naive Dynamic Resolution and M-ROPE; state-of-the-art on OCR benchmarks.
  • Molmo (Allen AI, 2024): Fully open weights and training data (PixMo dataset); strong pointing and grounding capability.
  • Pixtral 12B (Mistral, 2024): Native variable-resolution vision encoder trained from scratch alongside the LLM.

Landmark Models

NVIDIA NVLM: Three-in-One

  • NVIDIA’s NVLM (2024) uniquely provides three architecture variants in one model family:
    • NVLM-D (Decoder-only): Unified embedding — vision tokens fed directly into the LLM. Maximum training efficiency.
    • NVLM-X (Cross-attention): Visual features via cross-attention. Fewer vision tokens consumed by LLM; better high-res scaling.
    • NVLM-H (Hybrid): Combines both — global cross-attention for context + local embedding tokens for detail.
  • NVLM-72B matched or exceeded GPT-4V on key benchmarks while maintaining strong text-only performance.

Llama 3.2 Vision

  • Meta’s first open-weights multimodal model; 11B and 90B parameter variants.
  • Uses a pre-trained Llama 3.1 text backbone (frozen initially) with cross-attention layers inserted at regular intervals.
  • Vision encoder: pre-trained ViT adapted for Llama’s embedding space.
  • Training: first align the cross-attention layers, then do full instruction tuning.
  • Significance: Brought frontier multimodal capability to the open-source community.

Qwen2-VL: Native Resolution

  • Alibaba’s Qwen2-VL introduces Naive Dynamic Resolution (NDR): the model processes images at their native resolution rather than resizing to a fixed grid.
  • M-ROPE (Multi-dimensional Rotary Position Embedding): encodes 2D spatial position and temporal position for video, enabling the model to reason about spatial layout and time simultaneously.
  • Achieves state-of-the-art on document understanding, OCR, and mathematical reasoning benchmarks.
  • Handles videos up to 20 minutes natively.

Molmo: Fully Open

  • Allen AI’s Molmo (2024) stands out for being fully open: open weights, open training code, and open data.
  • PixMo dataset: 700k+ detailed image descriptions collected via human speech — avoids GPT-4 annotation bias.
  • Supports pointing: the model can point to specific regions in an image as part of its response.
  • Molmo-72B matches GPT-4V; Molmo-7B outperforms many closed models many times its size.
  • Demonstrates that open data + careful curation can rival proprietary annotation pipelines.

Pixtral 12B: Native Vision Encoder

  • Mistral’s Pixtral 12B (2024) trains its vision encoder from scratch alongside the LLM, rather than using CLIP.
  • Supports variable-resolution images: images are encoded at native resolution; no fixed grid needed.
  • Handles multiple images per prompt natively.
  • Achieves strong performance on multi-image reasoning and document understanding tasks.
  • Highlights a trend: moving away from CLIP as the universal vision encoder.

MLLM Applications

Healthcare & Life Sciences

  • Medical imaging analysis: MLLMs interpret chest X-rays, pathology slides, and MRI scans alongside clinical notes.
  • Clinical decision support: Cross-referencing patient-reported symptoms (text) with diagnostic images.
  • Drug discovery: Analyzing molecular structure images alongside scientific literature.
  • Key benefit: Reduces the siloing of image-reading specialists from text-based clinical reasoning systems.

Enterprise Productivity

  • Document intelligence: Extract, summarize, and answer questions from PDFs containing charts, tables, and mixed layouts.
  • Automated report generation: Narrate dashboards and data visualizations into executive summaries.
  • Manufacturing QA: Compare reference product images against production photos to detect defects automatically.
  • Code + diagram understanding: Parse architecture diagrams, flowcharts, and annotated screenshots as part of code review.

Disaster Response & Critical Infrastructure

  • Satellite imagery analysis: Identify flood extent, fire perimeter, or structural damage from aerial images combined with situation reports.
  • Energy grid monitoring: Combine sensor time-series data with visual inspection footage to predict equipment failure.
  • Fraud detection: Cross-reference document images (receipts, IDs) with transaction text records for multi-signal anomaly detection.

Evaluating MLLMs

Why MLLM Evaluation is Harder

  • Text LLM evaluation is already difficult. MLLMs add:
    • Cross-modal alignment: Does the model correctly ground its text response in the image/audio content?
    • Visual hallucination: Does the model describe objects, text, or relationships not present in the image?
    • Modality imbalance: Does the model over-rely on language priors and ignore the visual input?
  • Standard NLP metrics (BLEU, ROUGE) are insufficient — they cannot measure visual grounding at all.

Visual Hallucination: The Core MLLM Challenge

  • Object hallucination: The model describes objects not present in the image (e.g., “there is a cat” when there is none).
  • Attribute hallucination: Incorrect color, shape, count, or relationship between real objects.
  • Causes: Language priors dominate visual input; insufficient visual grounding in training.
  • POPE benchmark (Polling-based Object Probing Evaluation): asks Yes/No questions about object existence; compares adversarial vs. random sampling to expose hallucination patterns.

Key MLLM Benchmarks in Detail

  • MMMU (Massive Multidiscipline Multimodal Understanding): 11,500 college-level questions across 183 subfields requiring both image understanding and disciplinary knowledge.
  • MMBench: Bilingual (EN/CN) structured benchmark with 20 capability dimensions including object localization, social reasoning, and image quality assessment.
  • ScienceQA: Multi-hop reasoning questions from science curricula with diagrams; tests visual + factual reasoning together.
  • DocVQA: 50,000 questions on scanned documents — tests OCR, layout understanding, and information retrieval.

Applying Eval Engineering to MLLMs

  • The same 6-step Eval Engineering workflow applies to MLLMs — with an added visual dimension.
  • LLM-as-a-Judge for MLLMs: The judge receives the image, the prompt, the model response, and a rubric that explicitly scores visual grounding.
  • Key MLLM eval criteria to add to rubric:
    • Visual Faithfulness: Does the response accurately reflect what is in the image?
    • Hallucination Score: Are any claims made that cannot be verified from the image?
    • Modality Integration: Does the response synthesize visual and textual context, or ignore one?
  • Production guardrails for MLLMs should flag responses with low visual grounding scores.

Challenges & Future Directions

Hallucination & Trustworthiness

  • Hallucination is more severe in MLLMs than text-only LLMs — two modalities can independently mislead.
  • Mitigation strategies:
    • Contrastive decoding: penalize tokens likely under a “vision-blind” version of the model
    • Visual instruction tuning with negative examples
    • Post-hoc verification via a separate grounding model
  • Eval Engineering plays a critical role: deploy hallucination-specific LLM judges in production to catch failures before users do.

Computational Cost & Efficiency

  • A single high-resolution image can consume thousands of tokens in the LLM context.
  • Video inputs at even modest frame rates are prohibitively expensive for standard context windows.
  • Active research directions:
    • Efficient visual encoders (fewer tokens per image)
    • Dynamic resolution (only attend to high-detail regions)
    • Quantized vision encoders
    • Long-context architectures (Mamba, sliding window attention)

Modality Imbalance & Data Quality

  • Most MLLM training data is text-heavy — models develop strong language priors that can dominate visual reasoning.
  • High-quality paired multimodal datasets are expensive to curate (Molmo’s PixMo is a notable exception).
  • Cross-modal misalignment: image-text pairs from the web are noisy — captions often describe adjacent, not depicted, content.
  • Improving data quality (Molmo’s speech-annotation approach) consistently outperforms scale alone.

The Road Ahead: Omni-Modal Models

    • Text ↔︎ Image ↔︎ Audio ↔︎ Video ↔︎ Sensor data
